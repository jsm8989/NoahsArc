Gravity-related Experiments Training Week

Online training


FEBRUARY 17, 2021

Section 1: General overview

Earlier this month, our team completed the Gravity-Related Experiments Training Week (GRETW) provided by ESA as part of our preparation for the Spin Your Thesis! campaign coming up this September. We will be using this blog post to recount some of the week's experiences that we found the most useful and interesting! Science communication, although often overlooked, is an essential factor to informing, involving and inspiring people through STEM. We will be publishing more longform content in the coming months, and hope that you enjoy reading these alongside following our shorter updates over on Instagram and Twitter (both @teamnoahsarc).


Section 2: A Brief History of Gravity, and Astronaut Tales

Alongside our project workshops and team sessions, we attended two inspirational lectures: A Brief History of Gravity, by Professor Hendry, an astrophysicist at the University of Glasgow; and Tales from an Astronaut, by Jean-François Clervoy.

They were so full of interesting content that we have decided to dedicate a separate blog post to these. So stay tuned!


Section 3: What actually is Systems Engineering? Why do we require requirements?

Another great aspect of the training week was the level of interaction built into the lectures and activities, such as the Systems Engineering workshop. Delivered by Alex Kinnaird, Engineering Coordinator at the ESA Education Office, we were given a comprehensive overview of what systems engineering actually is, and how it is applied within the space industry.

Systems engineering is formally defined as an interdisciplinary field that deals with the design, integration and management of complex systems over their entire life cycle. Or, as Alex explained it - a bunch of things, which do a thing, which come together to do a specific thing (within a set boundary and a set time). The term ‘systems engineering’ has existed since the early 1900s, and its concepts were actually applied to major events such as World War II (see a more modern example below). This is often a surprising discovery for many, since the topic has only gained industry-wide traction and popularity in recent decades, spearheaded by milestones such as NASA’s publication of its Systems Engineering Handbook in 1995.

A ‘system’ that needs to be ‘engineered’ can be anything that involves multiple components (hardware, software, services or users), and connections between the same. For example, a city’s subway network is a system that includes the trains, passengers, route scheduling methods, ticketing systems, etc. These systems can also be examined at different levels of granularity, e.g. power transmission and door operation mechanisms are subsystems of a train, while turnstile design and available purchase methods can be subsystems of a ticketing process. One example of a relationship that connects all these listed components is the calculation of delays per unit time - older engines, complicated turnstiles and unautomated ticket purchasing can all increase the likelihood of delayed journeys, especially if the travel demand is high. Similarly, most space-domain technologies and missions are highly complex systems with many moving parts, which need to be engineered to work together effectively. This is where requirement writing and analysis comes in. Every subsystem and component is ‘required’ to fulfil certain functions, in order for their encompassing system to meet its overarching requirement or purpose (this is usually derived from project goals or user needs).


Each ‘Your Thesis!’ project has a requirement of successfully conducting their experiment, within the given temporal and financial boundaries. The main system within our project is the experiment itself, where the microscope and microfluidic chip setup will be located (see above). This interfaces with other systems such as the LDC platform/personnel, and the experiment control center. Formal, customized requirements need to be written for our experiment and assigned under various categories such as functional, performance and design.

Our learnings from this workshop were centered on writing good requirements, which we have been using in experimental documents that need to be completed before September’s campaign. Here are some examples of the requirements we have written so far:

  1. This experiment shall assess the influence of hypergravity on the passive glucose absorption process in cellular systems (functional)

  2. All experimental equipment in use shall be under the maximum mass of 800kg (LDC gondola capacity) on final setup (design)

  3. The fluid management system, including pumps, shall be powered using the LDC gondola’s existing power supply (interface)

This step is crucial since it enables us to ‘verify’ these requirements throughout our experiment’s lifecycle, i.e. check whether or not our developed system is meeting the established requirements. This is one of the ways that we can make our best effort to ensure success of the project.


Section 4: Speaking to the experts and solidifying our experiment

A significant part of this training week was made up of teamwork sessions on our projects, including the opportunity to quiz the experts from our respective facilities. We found this particularly valuable as it allowed us to clear up any technical questions we had about the Large Diameter Centrifuge, the dos and don’ts of running our experiment in it, and what type of biscuits we should bring the technicians. It is crucial to know the exact technical specifications and running parameters of any equipment we use, so that we can plan accordingly and (hopefully) minimise any risk of a failed experiment. Especially since our experiment day is the first time we will actually be using the LDC equipment! It also allowed us to listen to the questions of other Your Thesis! Teams, which brought up ideas we had not thought of, and discussions which were mutually beneficial to us all. By the end of the expert consultations, we left with a more solid plan, and a rather large shopping list of cables and components to purchase.

The importance of running multiple test experiments was driven home during these sessions. Even though we obviously cannot run pre-campaign tests in a hypergravity environment, we will be conducting the experiment itself in a lab environment and refining the procedure - in fact, Jamie will soon be practising some of the techniques in his home lab,  so that we can optimise our 2.5 days at the LDC. A combination of our learnings from this training week, and our knowledge from reading the LDC’s technical manual will enable us to prepare effectively for our campaign and pinpoint any other questions we may have for the relevant experts.
